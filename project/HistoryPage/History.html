Walk Through History

Roman Era

The history of Cluj actually begins with the Roman era. After the conquest of Dacia at the beginning of the 2nd century and its transformation into a province, Trajan established a settlement of colonists here. The Roman settlement of Napoca developed due to its advantageous strategic and commercial position, as it was located at the intersection of the main north-south and east-west salt trade routes in the region.

The Renaissance and Enlightenment

After the dissolution of the Hungarian kingdom in 1541 between the Ottoman Empire and the Austrian Empire, Cluj became part of the Principality of Transylvania. The first rulers of the autonomous principality were the wife (Queen Isabella) and the son (John Sigismund) of the last Hungarian king, John Zápolya. The existence of Transylvania as an autonomous principality created favorable conditions for development, and during this period, the city of Cluj became one of the most developed cities of the former Hungarian kingdom. In the absence of wars, although Alba Iulia was the political capital of the Transylvanian princes, Cluj became the economic, cultural, and religious center of the principality of Transylvania, consistently supporting the Transylvanian princes. In 1581, Stephen Báthory, the governor of Transylvania, founded a Jesuit academy here.

The Modern Era

At the end of the 17th century, following the Treaty of Karlowitz in 1699, Cluj became part of the Habsburg monarchy, coming under Austrian rule. After a forced agreement signed by Mihály Apafi I, the citadel of Cluj was required to host the troops of the Duke of Lorraine, providing them with a service of 100,000 florins. However, the soldiers also plundered the city and demanded additional payments from the taxpayers.

During The Second World War (1940–1945)

In 1940, Cluj came under Hungarian sovereignty through the Second Vienna Award. The Hungarian and German forces controlling the city were pushed back by Romanian and Soviet troops in October 1944. Through the Paris Peace Treaty of 1947, Cluj was once again integrated into Romania. After World War II, the Romanian government, together with the Allied Forces Commission, established a people's tribunal in Cluj to investigate individuals suspected of being war criminals, in accordance with Article 14 of the Armistice Agreement with Romania.

The Communist Period (1945–1989)

During the communist period, Cluj underwent an extensive process of industrialization and urbanization, with its population growing from approximately 115,000 in 1945 to around 320,000 in 1989. Through the systematization program, houses in the outskirts of Cluj were demolished and replaced by block housing neighborhoods such as Gheorgheni, Grigorescu, Între Lacuri, Mănăștur, Mărăști, Plopilor, and Zorilor.
